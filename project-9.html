Project 9

From virtual lab to a real-world system

Students explore how environmental data can inform real-world decisions by using GenAI as a virtual lab to simulate classroom conditions such as temperature and humidity. They visualise and critically analyse the generated data, identifying inconsistencies and reflecting on what reliable, realistic data should look like. Building on these insights, they design their own monitoring system, using AI as a thinking partner to propose practical, evidence-based solutions while developing data literacy, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills.

Full description

Students investigate (environmental) parameters in order to make specific evidence-based decisions. They use Gen AI as a virtual lab to simulate a dataset, critique the results to find flaws and finally act as designers to propose a real-world monitoring solution. Part 1: Students investigate the microclimate of their classroom (e.g. temperature and humidity) to observe the recorded data and make a specific decision (e.g., whether a particular plant could survive). As physical sensors may not be available or would take too much time to collect long-term data, students use Gen AI as a virtual lab to simulate a 24 or48-hour data log based on specific parameters (e.g, size of the room, location, season, orientation etc.). They provide the prompts to generate the dataset. Teachers help by providing a list of parameters to ensure that students include enough variables, thus making the dataset interesting. Part 2: Students visualize the AI-generated data using either a spreadsheet or a Gen AI tool for data visualization. They then critique this dataset looking for physical inconsistencies (e.g., unrealistic temperature or humidity levels). They document these inconsistencies, justify why they believe the data points are false based on specific principled/variables, and suggest how the data should appear in a real-world setting. Part 3: Based on the flaws identified in Part 2, students can create a “variables-protocol”. In this protocol they justify all the variables (dependent, independent, and controlled) that must be considered to ensure more reliable results and explain how these factors affect the final decision. They, then use Gen AI tools as a thinking partner to help them design a real-life monitoring system. Teachers can suggest prompts such as “Which sensors would be best for this task” and “Where should we place the sensors to get more accurate data”. Teachers can also encourage students to present their suggestions in the classroom.
