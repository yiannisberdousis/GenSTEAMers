Scenario 8

Our Screen Time: uncovering patterns and challenging assumptions

Students analyse screen time data from their own digital habits to identify patterns, detect anomalies, and draw evidence-based conclusions. Through working with real or anonymised datasets, they explore how and when devices are used, question assumptions, and compare different interpretations. Using GenAI as a thinking partner, students generate hypotheses, examine alternative explanations, and develop a more critical and informed understanding of their digital behaviour.

Possible Project Pathway

This project engages students in analysing data related to their own screen time and digital habits, helping them identify patterns, detect anomalies, and draw evidence-based conclusions. By working with personal or anonymised datasets, students develop data literacy and a more critical understanding of their digital behaviour.

Part 1 – Exploring Screen Time Data: Students are introduced to screen time data (from their own devices or a provided dataset) and explore what the data represents. They generate initial questions, such as when and how screen use varies throughout the day. Using GenAI as a hypothesis generator and explanation assistant, students formulate possible patterns as hypotheses, which they will later test.

Part 2 – Analysing Patterns in Data: Students analyse the dataset by calculating basic statistics (mean, median, mode, range) and creating graphs. They identify at least one trend (e.g. peak usage times), one anomaly, and one plausible but unsupported claim. GenAI is used as a pattern-spotter and thinking partner, suggesting alternative interpretations that students compare and critically evaluate.

Part 3 – Drawing Evidence-Based Conclusions: Students draw cautious conclusions based on their analysis, clearly distinguishing between what the data supports and what it does not. They identify assumptions and limitations, such as incomplete data or contextual factors. They compare datasets (e.g. weekdays vs weekends or different groups) and discuss possible explanations. GenAI supports this phase by generating what-if questions and alternative interpretations, which students assess and refine.

Part 4 – Questioning Assumptions and Reflecting on Data: Students reflect on how data can be interpreted in different ways and how conclusions depend on assumptions and context. They critically examine both their own reasoning and AI-generated outputs. GenAI acts as a critical thinking partner, helping students explore different perspectives while maintaining an evidence-based approach.

Part 5 – Communicating Insights: Students present their findings using clear data representations (graphs, short reports, or visual summaries). They justify their conclusions and explain the reasoning behind them, focusing on clarity, accuracy, and limitations. GenAI supports this phase as a communication assistant, helping structure explanations while ensuring that claims remain grounded in data.
